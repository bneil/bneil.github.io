Takeaways from Red Rising

Posted on Mar 4, 2026
article is 891 words and should take 5 minutes to consume 👀

Red Rising; Thoughts on the Society

I recently finished reading through the full Red Rising series, completing Light Bringer just a few days ago. After sitting with all of these wonderful books, I wanted to dig into some of the bigger topics the series raised for me.

I’ll try not to post spoilers, but that’s hard to avoid entirely. So be warned.

Is the Society Right?

I’m going to assume you have at least read Red Rising, in which we were introduced to a Society made up of Golds. A caste of humanoid that is the personification of the best physical modifications that could be grafted into the human genome. So superior, in fact, that dead Golds jettison their bodies to the sun to ensure that their DNA can’t be copied. More than the physical modifications, they are the top of a class system that ensures they are the “shepherds” of the other Colors.

Shepherding, which can mean a few things. Biblical meanings aside, morally, Golds consider themselves to be above the other Colors. As such, individuals act in a variety of ways. For my arguments, I’d push to say they fall into just a few camps:

  1. Your Golds that are hedonistic, yet bloodthirsty
  2. Your Golds that are basically sci-fi cotton farmers
  3. Your Golds that want to evolve art, culture, and civilization
  4. Your Golds that are conflicted. The ones who benefit from the system but feel the rot in it.

Some Golds are a mix of the above. But all in all, you have yourself a very privileged human.

Yet, throughout the books, I found it very telling that even alongside the absolute horror of human suffering those Golds maintained — the lower classes achieved wonders at the whip of their masters. The fantastical buildings on Mercury, the islands on Venus, even the Rim. I was amazed, and disgusted to say: maybe the Society, in terms of overall human progress, was in fact a force for good?

While the initial thought of slavery as a catalyst for humanity’s growth is grotesque, the idea itself is worth entertaining. Assuming you could have 1,000 years of abject servitude, what could a spacefaring culture create on the backs of their fellow man?

Think about what that moment of realization would feel like. You’ve spent your life, your parents spent theirs building something magnificent under duress. Then someone ‘breaks your chains’ and says: look around. This is yours. You made alllll this. Does pride come first, or rage? Can you feel both at the same time? I think Darrow wrestles with this exact tension, and it’s part of why his revolution is so messy. Because how could Darrow expect any of the newly freed people to take pride beneath that shadow.

So what would it mean to build something on the backs of others? As a Gold, what would it mean to have directed others in its construction? The pharaohs names are still memorable to this day. Not so much the builders of the pyramids, or the mathematicians who made them possible. That made me think about the legacy of the works that were accomplished.

You can see it today. The names on buildings are rarely the names of the people who built them. The Bezos and the Musks get the legacy; the warehouse workers and factory line engineers become footnotes. The scale is different, the cruelty is different, but the pattern isn’t new.

So was the Society right?

Even if the human race is run by hedonistic, murderous, overbearing parents… they got shit done. If you only value progress on the scale of humanity’s progress, it does feel like that organized leadership was effective. Regardless of the moral cost.

Is It Wrong, Though, If You Don’t Know?

But all of that assumes the enslaved know they’re enslaved. The moral calculus shifts entirely when you remove awareness from the equation.

I thought about the character of Cypher from the first Matrix movie. He was the character that backstabs the rebellion because he couldn’t stand living outside the Matrix. In much the same way, I felt the same Skinner box was provided to the low red Colors on Mars. Given their complete isolation from the whole of humanity, it felt like slavery, to them, wasn’t slavery. Much in the way npcs in the Matrix are batteries living fruitful lives, the drillers of Mars live and die providing helium live harsh but intimate lives.

So is it wrong to be subjugated if you are unaware? If you get to live an alright life, and your life’s work profits someone else? It’s still a good life. It’s the slavers who have the moral obligation to ensure you never find out, lest you be crushed under the true weight of the world.

What unsettles me most is that I can see the logic in it. I can trace the argument from point A to point B and nod along, right up until I remember that we’re talking about people. That’s the trick the Society pulls, and it’s the trick powerful systems have always pulled: make the math work, and people will forgive the human cost. Pierce Brown did a fantastic job that doesn’t let the reader sit comfortably with that. Its great writing in that sense and I found it a thrilling concept to noodle on.

Thanks for reading.
